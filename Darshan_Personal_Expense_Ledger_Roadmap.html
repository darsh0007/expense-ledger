Personal Expense Ledger & Social Debt Roadmap

1. Executive Summary

This project should not be treated as a normal budget tracker. Your credit card statement shows cash flow, while your monthly $800 limit should track personal consumption. These are different things.

Core thesis: Transactions are payment events. Allocations are ownership of consumption. Settlements are debt-clearing events. Your budget is calculated only from your personal allocations.

The app should let you import card transactions quickly, leave them in an inbox, and split them later when you have time. It must also handle the reverse case where someone else paid for something that belongs to you.

2. The Real-World Problem Model

What appears on your card

  • Personal spending
  • Groceries bought for the house
  • Items bought for someone else
  • Refunds and adjustments
  • Full shared bills that are not fully yours

What your budget should count

  • Your share of groceries
  • Your meals, coffee, transit, subscriptions
  • Your share of expenses paid by someone else
  • Manual cash expenses
  • Not the full shared bill

The app must preserve the difference between paid by me, consumed by me, owed to me, and owed by me.

3. Non-Negotiable Product Principles

Inbox first

You should be able to import transactions without splitting immediately. Busy life is a product requirement.

Budget from allocations

The $800 tracker must sum only your personal allocation lines, not raw credit card totals.

Manual entries matter

If someone else paid for you, it must still count against your budget even though it never touched your card.

Settlements are first-class

Cash, e-transfer, and offset repayments must be tracked explicitly rather than hidden as notes.

Splitwise is not the ledger

Your local ledger should be the source of truth. Splitwise is a communication/sync layer.

AI is just an input method

Voice/AI should call the same backend services as the UI.

4. Recommended Architecture

For a solo project, use a Next.js PWA + PostgreSQL + Prisma/Drizzle architecture. It keeps the web UI, backend routes, mobile-friendly app, and database access in one maintainable codebase.

7. Budget and Balance Rules

Budget calculation: monthly personal spend = sum of allocations where person is you and budget_impact is true within the budget period.
monthly_personal_spend = SUM(allocations.amount)
WHERE allocations.person_id = Darshan
AND allocations.budget_impact = true
AND allocation_date BETWEEN budget_period.start_date AND budget_period.end_date

8. Key User Flows

You paid for shared groceries

  1. CSV import creates a $120 Walmart transaction.
  2. Transaction appears in inbox.
  3. Later, allocate $45 to yourself, $40 to Logan, $35 to Rahul.
  4. Your budget increases by $45 only.
  5. Others' balances show amounts owed to you.
  6. Optional: push to Splitwise.

Someone else paid for you

  1. Manual entry: Rahul paid $32 dinner, your share $16.
  2. App creates a payable to Rahul.
  3. Your budget increases by $16.
  4. No credit card transaction is required.

Cash reimbursement

  1. Logan owes you $40.
  2. He gives you $25 cash.
  3. Record settlement: Logan to Darshan, $25, cash.
  4. Remaining balance: Logan owes $15.
  5. Your $800 budget does not change.

Offset settlement

  1. You owe Ankit $20.
  2. Ankit owes you $30.
  3. App shows net: Ankit owes you $10.
  4. Record offset or show net balance.

10. Phased Roadmap

Phase 0 - Ledger validation

Goal Prove the model handles messy life before building too much UI.

  • Write sample scenarios: shared groceries, cash repayment, someone paid for you, refund, partial settlement, offset, month boundary.
  • Confirm each scenario can be represented by transactions, allocations, and settlements.
  • Decide default categories and budget period.

Exit: You can manually calculate correct personal spend and balances from the model.

Phase 1 - Local MVP

  • Create Next.js project with Postgres and ORM.
  • Create tables: people, accounts, transactions, allocations, categories, budget_periods, imports.
  • Build CSV import with duplicate detection.
  • Build transaction inbox and allocation modal.
  • Build current month budget dashboard.
  • Add manual transaction entry.

Exit: Upload a statement, allocate later, and see true monthly spend.

Phase 2 - Settlements and balances

  • Add settlements table and UI.
  • Support cash, e-transfer, card, and offset settlements.
  • Build people balance page.
  • Support partial settlements and corrections.

Exit: You can answer who owes whom at any time.

Phase 3 - Splitwise sync

  • Add Splitwise identity mapping.
  • Add sync_events table.
  • Implement create expense for selected allocations.
  • Store external IDs, statuses, errors, retry data.

Exit: Local splits can be pushed safely and retried.

Phase 4 - Mobile-first PWA

  • Responsive dashboard and inbox.
  • Quick add manual expense flow.
  • Add to Home Screen support.
  • Optional offline draft entry.

Exit: Most quick events can be recorded from your phone.

Phase 5 - AI voice input

  • Use phone speech-to-text or transcription.
  • Parse natural language into structured JSON.
  • Show confirmation before saving.
  • Support commands like "Logan paid me $40 cash".

Exit: Common expense and settlement commands can be captured quickly.

Phase 6 - Bank sync only if still needed

  • Evaluate Plaid/open banking options carefully.
  • Implement account linking only after security tradeoffs are acceptable.
  • Use sync only to populate the same inbox.
  • Keep CSV as fallback.

11. What Not to Build Too Early

Do not start with Plaid

Bank sync adds auth, security, consent, webhook, and cost complexity. CSV is enough to validate.

Do not start with native mobile

A PWA likely solves the phone need first.

Do not let AI mutate data blindly

AI should draft structured entries and ask for confirmation.

Do not make Splitwise the ledger

Your budget logic should live locally.

12. Success Metrics

  • Upload a credit card CSV and deduplicate repeat imports.
  • Leave transactions unreviewed without breaking the budget.
  • Allocate one transaction across yourself and multiple people.
  • Record expenses someone else paid for you.
  • Record cash/e-transfer settlements.
  • See per-person net balances.
  • Use the app weekly without creating more work than it saves.

13. Final Recommendation

Build this, but build the ledger foundation first. The project becomes valuable as soon as it can answer: How much have I personally spent this month, and who owes whom?

Everything else - Splitwise, AI, mobile, and automatic bank import - should be layered on top of that stable foundation.
